Card system & rarity language

how every ability card is coded — read this first

Chaos adds ability cards on top of classic Rummikub (rules unchanged — first to empty their tile rack still wins). Cards are one-time use and there is no hand limit. Each card has a clean face — icon, name, one-line effect, and rarity, nothing else. Rarity reads two ways so it never leans on color alone: a distinct frame / foil per tier, backed by a single rarity word (Common / Uncommon / Rare). No letters, no pips.

White — Common
Parchment / ivory frame + the word COMMON. The bread-and-butter cards (Peek, Shield, Junk +2).
Blue — Uncommon
Deep table-blue tied to #13478f + the word UNCOMMON. Spicier effects (Junk +3, Wheel, Big-Wind).
Gold — Rare
Brass foil + the word RARE — the “wow” pull. Strongest cards (Junk +4, Skip, Lock).

Privacy rule baked in: you see your own card faces & count; opponents show only a subtle “has cards” aura near their avatar — never a number, never contents.

the key layout question

Your two holdings live on the wooden table-edge together but never blend: ivory tiles on the rack (left, the primary thing you act on), ability cards on their own brass-trimmed ledge (right). Different material + different shape = no crowding, no “is this a tile or a card?” confusion. Opponents around the felt show only a soft, always-on “has cards” aura — no count, no faces. The instant one of them gains a card the aura gives a single brief pulse (see Mara, “just drew”) and then settles back to its constant state.

Why a separate ledge? The tile rack is sacred — it’s the win condition and already crowded on a 4-player board. Bolting cards onto the rack would fight the tiles for width and muddle “what empties to win.” A distinct ledge keeps the two mental models clean and lets the card hand grow (no limit) without ever stealing tile space. Fan tidy-up: at rest only the front card shows its full face; the rest tuck into clean rarity-colored tabs (rarity still glanceable by color), and hovering fans them out — no label bleed.

Mobile-first treatment. A phone can’t fit two shelves. Cards become a pull-up drawer that overlays the rack. A thin peek-strip of card-tops pokes above the rack as the affordance (you can see your rarities at a glance); tapping the brass handle slides up a swipeable card row over the tiles. Tiles never lose space; cards are one tap away.

When an ability resolves, a gradient beam runs from the caster (gold node + “CASTER” pill) to the target. Shield — held badge & the block moment

Shield is a passive held card: instead of being “played,” it docks as an equipped badge near your rack. When the next ability targets you, it auto-spends and the attack fizzles — shown with a green block burst + toast (no timing/skill window; the server resolves it at commit).

🌀 Chaos mode — what’s new

  • Ability cards in three rarities (White / Blue / Gold) — one-time use, hidden from rivals.
  • A public random Wheel that fires on big plays — a little luck for the whole table.
  • Joker “bombs”: disturb a joker set and it may scatter across the board.
  • Bluff & challenge: play any card face-down and claim it’s something else.
  • Everything else is classic Rummikub — first to empty their rack still wins.
Two modes only (Classic / Chaos), matching the locked spec. The NEW badge + swirl icon flag the DLC; the coach intro defuses the “did the rules change?” worry while previewing what Chaos adds. Classic stays byte-for-byte the default path.

Joker danger — ambient tile heat

bonus · SP4 preview sketch

Any board set holding a joker is a “live bomb.” The danger lives in the joker tile’s own background color — cool ivory when safe, warming through amber, then hot red as it climbs (20% → 80%) — and the joker’s face morphs right along with it: calm → wary → annoyed → furious. The precise odds appear only on hover, so the felt stays calm until you’re actually deciding whether to touch it.

How Chaos works

Classic Rummikub still applies — empty your tile rack to win. On your turn you may also play ability cards (one-time, hidden from rivals). Some aim at one player; some hit everyone or the table. Any card can be played face-down with a bluff, and the target (or table) may challenge.
